Evaluation Simulation: Soapbottle vs. Meta

Kate Wang - Penn State University

EBIT for Soapbottle

Gross Income Assumptions

  • Consumer Growth: Linear projection from Kickstarter data (913 new users/month).
  • Market Growth: 7.2% CAGR (Organic Soap Market).
  • Usage: 1 bottle per consumer per year.
  • Price: 13 euros per 100ml bottle.

Cost Assumptions

  • Material: 1.69 euros per soap.
  • Labor: $10/hour for one worker.
  • Facilities: 10 machines at $254/machine.

Accountant Adjustment for Soapbottle

To get to Free Cash Flow, we need to adjust for:

  • PA Tax Rate
  • Depreciation on the 10 machines
  • Changes in Inventory (samples for ads, inventory for orders)
  • Capital Expenditure on the machines

Discount Factor (WACC) for Soapbottle

To calculate WACC, we need to estimate:

  • Long-term treasury bond yield of the country
  • Beta (a measure of the project's risk)
  • Equity risk premium
  • Pre-tax interest rate (cost of debt)
  • Corporate tax rate

Termination Value for Soapbottle

The perpetual growth rate (g) for the terminal value calculation is assumed to be the industry average growth rate.

Key Takeaways: Evaluation Simulation

Valuation in Practice:

  • Context Matters: The assumptions for a startup (Soapbottle) are vastly different from an established tech giant (Meta).
  • Data is Imperfect: Valuations often rely on projections and industry benchmarks, which carry inherent uncertainty.
  • Startups vs. Giants: Estimating growth for a startup might involve market sizing and adoption curves, while for a giant like Meta, it involves analyzing existing revenue streams and new ventures.

Strategic Implications:

  • Financial evaluation is not just a mechanical exercise; it's a strategic tool to compare different innovation projects.
  • The process forces a detailed examination of a project's potential risks and rewards.

The true value of the NPV exercise lies in the strategic thinking it provokes, not just the final number.
