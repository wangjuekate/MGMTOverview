Preemption Regulations and Venture Growth: Transcendent Technological Framing as Legitimacy Buffering

Kate Jue Wang

Penn State University

When ventures are faced with the need to change their strategies, they modify their narratives to maintain legitimacy

Narrative Continuity

Goal: Reassure existing stakeholders

• Identity anchoring (Navis & Glynn, 2011)

Reiterate core mission despite strategy changes

Tesla: "accelerating sustainable energy"

• Retrospective sensemaking (Kaplan & Orlikowski, 2013)

Frame changes as natural progressions

Health-tech: "deepening commitment to healthcare"

Narrative Adaptation

Goal: Attract new stakeholders

• Narrative reframing (Garud et al., 2014)

Adjust "how" while keeping "why"

Drone: "aerial photography" → "infrastructure monitoring"

• Bridging framing (Snow & Benford, 1988)

Extend narratives without alienating originals

Fintech: "democratizing" → "inclusive global finance"

Contextualization: grounding narratives in local contexts to build legitimacy

Dilemma: Contextualization vs. Expansion into New Context

Locally grounded narratives build legitimacy but may deter investors seeking scalable, cross-jurisdiction, cross-cultural growth.

• Japanese drone firms (e.g., Zipline Japan, ACSL): Emphasize safety and community trust—credible at home, hard to scale abroad.

• German health-tech ventures (e.g., Ada Health): Anchor on GDPR and national systems—trusted locally, less transferable globally.

Preemption Regulations Exacerbate Dilemma

Definition: State or federal laws that limit local governments' authority to regulate emerging industries (Riverstone-Newell, 2017)

Example: 16 states enacted drone preemption laws by 2019, overriding hundreds of local ordinances

Present Challenge

• Operating under fragmented local regulations

• Each jurisdiction has distinct requirements

Future Challenge

• Anticipating centralized regulation

• Need narratives that travel across jurisdictions

The Dilemma: Contextualized narratives that work locally fail to demonstrate scalability needed to attract investment in anticipation of regulatory centralization

Research Question

How do ventures handle the dilemma exacebated by preemption regulations ?

Transcendent Technological Framing

Definition: A narrative strategy linking technologies to moral intuitions that make ventures appear compliant and trustworthy across different regulatory regimes

Theoretical Origins: Extends transcendent framing from social movement theory (Snow & Benford, 1988)

Activists use it to mobilize diverse stakeholders by appealing to higher-order values

Moral Intuition Theory: MFT proposes a small set of evolved “foundations” (e.g., care/harm, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity) that appear across cultures, with local variation in emphasis. Cross-cultural structure has supportive (and some critical/nuanced) evidence (Haidt, 2001; Graham et al., 2011)

Transcendent technological framing examples:

Aibnb tie their technologies to portraying “belonging anywhere” (loyalty/betrayal) to transcend cultural differences.

Beyond Meat positioning around “sustainability and health” (care/harm) rather than culture-specific diets.

Drone ventures tying their technologies to safety and security (care/harm) of data to transcend local regulations.

Transcendent Framing help handle the dilemma exacebated by preemption regulations

Transcendent Framing increases cognitive resonance across institutional contexts by abstracting away from idiosyncratic local norms toward universal values recognizable in multiple jurisdictions (Meyer & Rowan, 1977; Scott, 2014)

Transcendent framing supports scalability imagination—the perception that a technology can operate consistently across different institutional environments (Garud, Gehman, & Giuliani, 2014)

Transcendent framing provides legitimacy buffering against audiences in the home market (Pollock & Rindova, 2003)

Theoretical Model

Moderator 1: Homogenous Moral Foundations Across Neighboring States

Moral foundations—such as care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, and sanctity/degradation—vary in their relative salience (Haidt, 2001; Graham et al., 2011)

When neighboring jurisdictions have homogenous moral foundations, ventures can use transcendent framing to appeal to shared values

Moderator 2: Technological Complexity

Key Idea: Complex technologies amplify reliance on moral framing over technical details

When technologies are complex or hard to evaluate, audiences rely on moral framing to assess legitimacy (Rosa et al., 1999; Kennedy, 2008)

Moral abstractions compensate for technical opacity by providing interpretable trust markers (Suchman, 1995)

Data and Sample: UAS (Drone) Industry, 2014-2019

Sample

684 drone ventures tracked across 3,574 firm-year observations

16 states enacted preemption legislation by 2019

Data Sources

Venture narratives: Collected from websites using Wayback Machine

State preemption laws: State Legislation Association

Homogenous moral foundations : Neighboring state race from Census database

Technological complexity: USPTO patent subclasses

Variables

Dependent Variable: Transcendent Technological Framing

Definition: Proportion of technology-related sentences that link the technology to moral purposes

Measurement: Sentences coded as 1 if they (a) contain moral intuition terms from Moral Foundations Dictionary, and (b) refer to the firm's drone technology

Examples of moral intuition terms: "safety," "care," "protection," "fairness," "responsibility"

Illustration of Transcendent Framing versus Non-transcendent Framing

Transcendent Framing:Zipline — frames autonomy around care, fairness, and saving lives (“build the world’s first logistics system that serves all people equally”; medical delivery at hospitals/clinics).

Non-transcendent Framing:Skydio — articulates “responsible use” and a mission to make the world safer with autonomous flight, foregrounding privacy/civil liberties.

Non-transcendent Framing:DJI — flagship pages emphasize products, imaging specs, and performance across segments; the pitch is mostly capability-driven rather than moral-value claims.

Independent Variable: Preemption Regulations

Measuring Homogenous Moral Foundations

Racial homogeneity as a proxy

Measuring Technological Complexity

Overlap of patent subclasses for each venture

Lower overlap = technologies span diverse, unrelated domains = higher systemic complexity

Control Variables

Venture-level

• Prior investments (dummy) | Lobbyists hired in state | Patents held by venture

• Political ties (membership in FAA/state regulatory collaborations)

• Prior narrative sentiment (Stanford NLP sentiment score)

County-level

• Local competition (# registered drones in county)

• City regulation restrictiveness (# restrictive clauses)

• State regulation restrictiveness (# restrictive clauses)

• Protests against drones (count from LexisNexis)

Fixed effects

Venture fixed effects + Year fixed effects

Key Findings

1. Preemption regulations increase ventures' adoption of transcendent technological framing—linking technologies to universal moral purposes (safety, fairness, welfare) by 19%

2. Effect is stronger when: (a) Moral foundations across neighboring jurisdictions is more homogenous (5.4%), and (b) technologies are more complex (harder to evaluate technically) (8.6%)

3. Transcendent framing attracts more investment ($2,300 increase) and increases number of innovation (20% increase)
